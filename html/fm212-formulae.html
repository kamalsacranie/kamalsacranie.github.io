    Market risk

    Risk is the variance of the returns of an instrument
    Expected returnThe expected return on an asset is the weighted average rate of return of all possible outcomes, where the weights are the probabilities ($p$) $E(R)=p_1R_1+p_2R_2+\dots+p_NR_N$
    $\rArr E(R)=\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{N}p_iR_i$
    Sample mean return: $\overline{R}=$ $\dfrac{1}{N}$$N$ is the number of observations in the sample$\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{N}R_i$
    Variance of returns $\sigma^2=p_1\left[\R_1-E(R)\right]^2+p_2\left[\R_2-E(R)\right]^2+\dots+p_N\left[\R_N-E(R)\right]^2$
    $\rArr$ $\sigma_R^2$Standard deviation: $\sigma_r=\sqrt{\sigma_R^2}$ $=\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^Np_i[R_i-E(R)]^2$
    Sample variance and standard deviation $ s(R)^2=\dfrac{1}{N-1}\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^N\left(R_i-\overline{R}\right)^2 $
    CovarianceCalculates how two stocks' variances associate ($X$ and $Y$ in this case) $\sigma_{X,Y}$Represents the covariance of $X$ and $Y$ $=E[(X$ $-$ $E(X)$Represents the expected return on $X$$)(Y-E(Y))]$
    Sample covaviance $Cov(X,Y)=\dfrac{1}{N-1}\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^N\left(X_i-\overline{X}\right)\left(Y_i-\overline{Y}\right)$
    Correlation $\rho_{X, Y}$$-1<\rho<1$ $=\dfrac{\sigma_{X,Y}}{\sigma_X\sigma_Y}$
    Sample correlation $Corr(X,Y)=\dfrac{Cov(X,Y)}{s(X)s(Y)}$
    Expected return on a portfolio $E(R_p)=w_1E(R_1)+w_2E(R_2)+\dots+w_NE(R_N)$The return on a portfolio is equal to the expected return on all the stocks in the portfolio.
    $E(R_p)=\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^Nw_iE(R_i),\qquad$where $\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^Nw_i=1$
    Variance of a portfolio with 2 assets $\sigma_{R_p}^2=w_1^2\sigma_{R_1}^2+w_2^2\sigma_{R_2}^2+2w_1w_2Cov(R_1,R_2)=\sigma_{R_p}^2=w_1^2\sigma_{R_1}^2+w_2^2\sigma_{R_2}^2+2w_1w_2\sigma_1\sigma_2Corr(R_1,R_2)$
    Variance of portfolio with $N$ assets $\sigma_{R_p}^2=\left(\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^Nw_n^2\sigma_{R_n}^2\right)+\left(\underset{m\ne n}{\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^N\displaystyle\sum_{m=1}^N}w_nw_mCov(R_n,R_m)\right)$
    • Diversification at a glanceAllows us to see that variance decreases with the number of stocks but covariance increases: $\sigma^2_{R_p}=\dfrac1N$$\overline{\sigma^2}$This is the average variance of the securities in the portfolio defined by: $\overline{\sigma^2}=\dfrac1N\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^N\sigma_{R_n}^2$ $+$ $\left(1-\dfrac1N\overline{Cov}\right)$The average covariance defined by: $\overline{Cov}=\dfrac{1}{N(N-1)}\underset{m\ne n}{\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^N\displaystyle\sum_{m=1}^N}Cov(R_n,R_m)$. There are $N(N-1)$ covariance terms in a portfolio of $N$ instruments
    Average variance and covariance
    1. Average variance: $\overline{\sigma^2}=\dfrac1N\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^N\sigma_{R_n}^2$
    2. Average covariance: $\overline{Cov}=\dfrac{1}{N(N-1)}\underset{m\ne n}{\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^N\displaystyle\sum_{m=1}^N}Cov(R_n,R_m)$
    Beta $\beta_i=$ $\dfrac{Cov(R_i,R_p)}{\sigma^2_{R_p}}$$R_i$ is the stock you're analysing and $R_p$ is your portfolio or the entire market $M$ $=\rho \dfrac{\sigma_R}{\sigma_M}$

    Portfolio theory
    Sharpe ratio $\text{Sharpe ratio}=$ $\dfrac{E(r_A)-r_{r_f}}{\sigma_{r_A}}$Where $E(r_A)$ is the expected return on a portfolio consisting of risky assets and a risk free asset. $r_{r_f} and $\sigma_{R_A}$ is the variance of the portfolio previously mentioned$
    CAPMThe expected return of an asset is the risk free rate plus the market risk premium times the asset's beta
    • When a stock has a higher expected return than when calculated by CAPM, we consider that stock to be generating abnormal returns, AKA, the stock is current underpriced, and vice versa. The distance between the abnormal returns and the CAPM line is known as $\alpha$
    		$E(r_i)=r_{r_f}+\beta_i$$[E(r_M)-r_{r_f}]$This is knows as the market premium. It is the expected return on the market above the risk free rate. This is also the gradient of the secure market line in space ($E(R_M),\beta$)
    Rearranged CAPM with alpha residual $E(r_i)-r_{r_f}=\alpha+\beta_i[E(R_m)-r_{r_f}]$
    Expected return using the share ratio (CMLCapital market line) $E(r_i)=$ $r_{r_f}+\text{Sharpe}\times\sigma$Here the risk free rate functions as the $y$-intercept and the $\sigma$ as our dependent variable

    Put and call options
    Put-call parity without dividends $C_0$Price of the contract $+$ $\dfrac{K}{(1+r_{r_f})^T}$This is the present value of the strike price $K$. This is the price at which you're allowed to buy a call stock or sell a put stock $=$ $S_0$ $+$ $P_0$Put option premium (the contract)
    Put-call parity with dividends $ S_0+P_0=C_0+PV(K)+PV(\text{DIVS}) $
    Replicating portfolio call pricing:
    • DeltaThis is basically the gradient of the asset replication line.: $\Delta=$ $\dfrac{C_u-C_d}{S_0(u-d)}$Where $C_u$ is the value of the call in the up branch and $C_d$ is the value of the call in the down branch. $S_0$ is the price of the underlying assets at $t=0$ and $u,\ d$ are % the underlying assets changes for each branch
    • BetaThis is the amount you need to borrow at the risk free rate to replicate this portfolio: $\Beta=\dfrac{C_d(u)-C_u(d)}{u-d}$
    • Replicating portfolio: $C=S_0(\Delta)+\dfrac{\Beta}{1+r_{r_f}}$
    Replicating portfolio put pricing $P=S_0(\Delta)+\dfrac{\Beta}{1+r_{r_f}}$Here we are SHORTING the stock and BORROWING the $\Beta$
    Risk-neutral pricing method $C=$ $\dfrac{qC_u+(1-q)C_d}{1+r_{r_f}}$Where $q$ is the risk neutral probability of a move up and $1-q$ the risk neutral of a move down$,\qquad$ where $q$ is:
    $\quad q=$ $\dfrac{1+r_{r_f}-d}{u-d}$Where $d$ is the actual coefficient you would times the share price by to get the share price in the down state and $u$ is the opposite.

    Payout and debt policy
    Cum dividend-ex dividend equality $P_{cum}-P_{ex}=d \ \times$ $\left(\dfrac{1-\tau_d}{1-\tau_{cg}}\right)$Where $\tau_cg$ is the tax on capital gains and $\tau_d$ is the tax on dividends. I.e., the price of the share before the dividend less after dividend should equal the dividend times the ratio of 1 - taxes
    Leverage ratio $\text{Leverage ratio}=\dfrac{\text{Debt}}{\underset{\textbf{(Debt + Equity)}}{\text{Assets}}}$
    MM proposition 1
    • If capital markets are doing their job, firms cannot increase value by changing their capital structure
    • Firm value is independent of the debt ratio
    • Capital structure is irrelevant, even if that debt is risky
    • $A$The market value of a firm's assets $=$ $V_u$The value of the firm if unlevered. $V_U=\dfrac{E(\text{future cash flows})}{(1+r_a)}$ $=$ $V_L$Value of the firm if levered $=$ $D+E_L$The market value of the firm's debt and equity if it is levered
    With this last point, if firms are identical and you invest the same proportion in any column of the equation, it should yield the same return
    MM proposition 2 (the basis of WACC) A firm's $r_E$ increases in proportion to the DE ratio. An increase in expected return is exactly offset by an increase in risk.
    WACCWeighted average cost of capital $WACC=r_U=\left(\dfrac{D}{V}\right)r_D+\left(\dfrac{E}{V}\right)r_E\qquad$ or we can rearrange to get $\qquad r_E=r_u+\dfrac{D}{E}(r_U-r_D)$The expected return on equity of a levered firm is the expected return on the unlevered firm plus the difference of the expected return on unlevered and debt, multiplied by the debt to equity ratio. The expected rate of return on the common stock of a levered firm increases in proportion to the debt-equity ratio but the increase in return is offset by the increase in risk
    MM 2 in CAPM Betas $\beta_E=\beta_U+\left(\dfrac{D}{E}\right)(\beta_U-\beta_D)$
    Interest tax shield $\text{Interest tax shield}$ $=$ $(D\times r_d)\times\tau_c$Interest payed times the tax rate gets us the amount of tax we didnt pay due to the deduction of interest due to debt investors. This is only used when there are not bankruptcy costs
    After-tax WACC $WACC_{AT}=r_D(1-\tau_c)\left(\dfrac DV\right)+r_E\left(\dfrac{E}{V}\right)$
    Adjusted present value $APV=\text{base case NPV}+\sum\text{PV of financing side effects}$
    Present value of interest tax shield $PV(ITS)=\dfrac{D\times r_D\times\tau_c}{r_D}=D\times\tau_c$
    Relative advantage of debt $RAD=\dfrac{1-\tau_p}{(1-\tau_{pE})(1-\tau_c)}$If this is greater than 1, the we issue debt because the after tax income to debtors is higher than investors. $\tau_p$ here is the tax applicable to a person who receives an interest payment and $\tau_{pE}$ is the personal tax used for dividends.
    Trade-off theoryThe optimal leverage a firm should have
    • APV formula: $V_L$Levered value of the project $=$ $V_U+PV(\text{tax shields})-PV(\text{cost of financial distress})$

    • Positive covenant: specify action that the firm must take like requiring EBIT to be above a minimum
    • Negative covenants: limits of forbids actions that the firm might usually take like Taking more senior debt on than current debt, dividend restrictions
    Callable bond
    • Call provisions: gives the issuer the option to redeem or retire the debt early
    • Callable bondThis allows firms to adjust capital structure (by lowering leverage) and take advantage of lower interest rates: a bond that can be repurchased (called) by the firm before maturity at a specified price (call price)
    • Valuation:$P_t^{\text{callable}}=P_t^{\text{straight}}$ $-$ $c_t$The call option value

    • Conversion price: value of the bond divided by the number of shares into which it may be exchanged. The conversion price will be quoted in terms of the market value of thee bond (market conversion price)

    • Valuation: $P_t^{\text{convertible}}=P_t^{\text{straight}}$ $+$ $c_t$Added because the underlying assets are shares. THis is the value of the call option to aquire common stock
    Warrants Call options written by the company on new stock. Iti is similar to a package of a straight bond and a warrant. Warrants increase the number of shares outstanding if exercised and can be issued on their own

    Different forms of market efficiency
    • Weak form: investors cannot predict abnormal returns given observed history of past stock prices, trading volume and other trading data.
    • Semi-strong form: investors cannot predict abnormal returns given publicly avail- able information (management quality, balance sheet composition, accounting performance, corporate news etc)
    • Strong form: investors cannot predict abnormal returns using publicly and privately available information.
